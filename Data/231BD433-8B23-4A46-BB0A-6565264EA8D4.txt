data rate transmission at high-mobility environments. 
For the channel estimation, the compressive sensing 
technique is applied to estimate sparse channels due 
to the high data rate transmission (or high sampling 
rate). The color noise resulted from the compressive 
matrix, the channel sparse feature as well as the 
channel variation due to the Dopper effect are 
jointly taken into account to formulate a Bayesian 
convex optimization problem. The provided channel 
estimation method can effectively improve the channel 
estimation performance and save the number of pilot 
subcarriers in fast time-variant sparse multipath 
channels. For the channel equalization, we propose a 
multiuser minimum mean square error (MU-MMSE) 
equalizer to obtain the initial data detection, 
followed by an inter-carrier interference canceller 
and a single-user MMSE (SU-MMSE) equalizer to improve 
the bit error rate in an iterative manner. For the 
subcarrier resource scheduling, we first discuss the 
influence of two difference standardized subcarrier 
mapping approaches in LTE, localized and distributed 
mapping, on the bit error rate (BER) performance 
where users could have different vehicular speeds. 
Further, we analyze the pairwise error probability in 
terms of the subcarrier mapping matrix and provide an 
optimum subcarrier mapping strategy for mobile 
environments.

Abstractâ€”In this paper, we investigate the service coverage for
interweaving cognitive radio networks with cooperative relays in
shadowed areas within the metropolitan region. We highlight
the influence of relays on the primary and secondary systems
in severe shadow fading channels. The outage probability is
utilized as a comprehensive performance metric to characterize
the coverage quality for the cognitive radio system with relays.
We show that cooperative spectrum sensing among the secondary
transmitter and relays can improve the vacant spectrum detection
probability and the false alarm probability, thereby making the
additionally introduced interference by relays to be constrained
within a tolerable limit. Overall, our analysis unveils that both
the spectrum utilization rate and the spatial coverage can be
significantly improved through the use of the relays, and there
exists the spatial selectivity phenomena for both the primary and
secondary systems.
I. INTRODUCTION
With the growth in wireless services, the demand for
spectrum resource is rapidly increasing. The current spectrum
allocation policy is static and inefficient to utilize the precious
spectrum resource. Recently, cognitive radio has been deemed
as an effective method to improve the spectrum utilization by
sharing the underutilized spectrum owned by primary license
users with secondary users through the dynamic spectrum
access [1]. Based on the required knowledge between the
primary and secondary systems, cognitive radio can be broadly
categorized into three types: interweave, underlay, and overlay
[2]. Basically, the spectrum sensing capability and the trans-
mission opportunity exploitation are two critical challenges
in designing an interweaving cognitive radio network. On
one hand, the hidden terminal problem for monitoring the
vacant spectrum is a major challenge for the spectrum sensing,
particularly when the cognitive radio performance degrades
from severe shadow fading. Cooperative spectrum sensing
has been proposed greatly increasing the ability to sense the
unused spectrum [3][4]. On the other hand, the heterogeneity
of spectrum availability and traffic demand among secondary
users has brought new challenge for efficient spectrum allo-
cation and transmission opportunity exploitation in cognitive
radio networks [5].
Providing service to cognitive users within urban areas
will be one of the important applications due to the ever
increasing demand for higher spectrum utilization. However,
within most of the urban areas, the severe shadowing will
bring new difficulty for deploying cognitive radio networks
since channel variations could be ranged with ten decibels. The
relaying technique seems to be a natural choice to assist the
cognitive user to overcome the two aforementioned challenges,
namely to improve the spectrum sensing capability and the
transmission opportunity exploitation.
In recent years, cooperative spectrum sensing has drawn a
lot of research attention [3][4][6]â€“[7]. The optimal cooperative
spectrum sensing methods to maximize channel throughput
are discussed in [6], and the relay-based cooperative spectrum
sensing methods over independent Rayleigh fading channels
are presented in [7]. It should be addressed that, all the
previous works do not consider the cooperative spectrum
sensing in severe shadowing environments. Moreover, the
extent of interference caused by multiple secondary users to
the primary user is another research issue in the design of
the cognitive radio network [8]â€“[10]. The analysis in [8]â€“[10]
assumes that all the cognitive transmitter-receiver pairs are
away from the primary transmitter with a certain protection
range to limit the interference to the primary users. The
protection range may help limit the introduced interference to
the primary user, while it may confine the spectrum sensing
capability, and therefore reduce the transmission opportunity
for the secondary users. Furthermore, our analysis in this
paper discovers that given the distance between the primary
and secondary transmitters, together with the placement of
relays around the secondary transmitter, the guaranteed service
coverage region in terms of some required outage probability
for the primary user is not a circle region around the primary
transmitter due to the spatial selectivity phenomena. Hence,
the use of a strict protection circle region may not be the best
setup in terms of minimizing the interference and maximizing
the spectral efficiency for both the primary user and the
interweaving cognitive radio networks, respectively.
In this paper, we will investigate the cognitive radio net-
work with cooperative relays for the shadowed-area service
coverage, and intend to answer the following questions: (1)
how much additional interference will be introduced to the
primary system; (2) how much improvement will be achieved
for the secondary system. We will first analyze the perfor-
mance of the cooperative spectrum sensing with multiple
relays in shadow fading channels. The successful decoding
probability for the decode-and-forward (DF)-based relays is

outage probability for the primary user. This figure unveils the
spatial selectivity phenomenon for the outage probability. For
ğ‘‘ğ‘ƒğ‘‡ ,ğ‘ƒğ‘… = 20, we can see that when there is no relay, 80%

away from the secondary transmitter can have better outage
probability performance. Also, we can find that the guaranteed
service coverage in terms of the desired outage probability, i.e.,
protection region, for the primary user is not a circle region
around the primary transmitter.
Fig. 5 and Fig. 6 show the outage probability for the

deployment of relays, the spatial coverage for the secondary
