Improved Miller SRM Driver with 
Continuous-Conduction-Mode Charge-Pump Power Factor 
Correction  
    This invention is concerned about an ameliorated Miller 
switched reluctance Motor driver that has Continuous Current 
Mode of charge pump power factor corrector. Said it at least 
comprises one AC input power, one charge pump power factor 
corrector of continuous current mode, and a Miller switched 
reluctance Motor driver. At first, said to utilize charge pump 
power factor corrector to filter the noise of high frequency 
which is from the AC input power. Then transform AC to DC, 
meanwhile to store or release AC, to adjust power factor of AC 
input power, and let the current which pass through the factor 
corrected inductor become continuous current mode. Finally, 
said in order to providing power for the switched reluctance 
motor, utilize Miller switched reluctance Motor driver to make 
the DC change to AC output power. Moreover, let the switched 
reluctance motor release power expeditiously.

Abstract-- This paper presents the system analysis of the 
continuous-conduction mode (CCM) dual-loop controlled 
light-emitting-diode (LED) boost driver. In order to predict the 
system performance, including loop gain, line regulation and 
load regulation, the equivalent circuit models of the LED boost 
drivers are constructed to derive the system transfer functions. 
Based on the given circuit specifications and parameters, the 
calculated results are compared with the SIMPLISÂ® 
simulations and measured results to verify the derived transfer 
functions. 
 
Index Terms-- Boost converter, Continuous-conduction 
mode, Driver circuits, Dual-loop control, 
Lighting-emitting-diode, Modeling, System analysis. 
I. INTRODUCTION 
Since the first light-emitting-diode (LED) was developed 
in 1962 [1], continuing developments in LED technology 
have given them several advantages over incandescent, 
fluorescent, and discharge light sources, in terms of longer 
lifetime, smaller size and faster response [2]-[5]. For the 
LCD backlights in mobile phones and personal digital 
assistants (PDAs), LEDs are connected in series, which 
requires a higher driving voltage level. However, most 
portable devices are powered by single lithium-ion battery 
packs, with voltage levels ranging from 2.5 V to 4.2 V [6]. 
Therefore, an LED driver in a portable device must step up 
the voltage level for driving the high-voltage LED string. 
The continuous-conduction mode (CCM) dual-loop 
controlled LED boost drivers [7], as shown in Fig. 1, 
developed in previous research work [8]-[10], have been 
widely employed in many LED driver integrated circuits 
(ICs) [11], [12]. The dual-loop control scheme is composed 
of two current-feedback loops. The outer loop senses the 
average current with resistor RFB in series with the LED 
string for constant-current regulation. The inner loop senses 
the peak current from the switch for the peak-current-mode 
control [13]-[15], which provides several benefits, such as 
the feed-forward and current-limiting functions. The 
three-input comparator and the theoretical waveforms of the 
dual-loop control scheme are shown in Fig. 2, in which the 
switch current signal is summed with the external ramp and 
compared with the control signal Vc to provide the duty 
cycle to the power stage.

Abstract-- This paper presents the equivalent circuit models 
of the light-emitting-diode, including the piece-wise linear 
model, DC model and small-signal model. In order to 
characterize the equivalent circuit model of the LEDs, a 
piece-wise linear model is built by adopting the superposition 
concept of the multi-branch equivalent circuit model. 
Furthermore, in order to perform the DC and small-signal 
analysis of the LEDs, the equivalent DC and small-signal 
resistance will be derived from the piece-wise linear model. 
 
Index Terms-- Equivalent circuit model, Light-emitting-diode, 
Lighting driver, Piecewise linear approximation, System 
analysis.

V. CONCLUSIONS 
This paper has proposed a piece-wise linear model instead 
of the approximate linear model to more precisely 
characterize the equivalent circuit model of the LED. The 
piece-wise linear model consist of more branches to describe 
the V-I curve of the LEDs. The comparisons of the measured 
and modeled results shown that the piece-wise linear model 
matches the measured V-I curve much better than the 
approximate linear model. Furthermore, the equivalent DC 
and small-signal models of the LED have been derived from 
the proposed piece-wise linear model. The experimental 
results shown that the calculated and measured small-signal 
resistances of the LED fairly match with each other.

IV. CONCLUSIONS 
This paper has presented the system analysis of the DCM 
dual-loop controlled LED boost driver. The equivalent 
circuit models of the DCM dual-loop controlled LED boost 
driver have been built. The system transfer functions have 
been derived to predict the system performance, including 
loop gain, line-to-output gain and output impedance. 
Finally, based on the given circuit specifications and 
parameters, the system transfer functions have been 
calculated to plot the Bode diagrams for the comparison 
with the SIMPLISÂ® simulations and experimental results.

Conclusions
1. The small-signal characteristics of boost converters for driving 
resistive load and LED load are different.
2. The equivalent DC and small-signal resistances of LEDs can be applied 
instead of the resistive load for DC and small-signal analysis.
3. System performance of LED boost driver can be predicted from 
the built DC and small-signal model.
4. Predicted, simulated and measured Bode diagrams of LED boost 
driver are in close agreement.

The present invention relates to a Miller switched reluctance motor
driver with continuous-current-mode charge-pump power factor
correction. The Miller switched reluctance motor driver system
includes an input AC voltage source, a continuous-current-mode charge
-pump power-factor correction circuit, and a Miller switched
reluctance motor driver. The continuous-current-mode charge-pump
power-factor correction circuit for the Miller switched reluctance
motor driver is utilized to achieve high power-factor. Furthermore,
by using the auxiliary inductor and capacitor, the phase inductor of
the Miller switched reluctance motor driver discharges quickly in
order to have low power losses and high conversion efficiency.
