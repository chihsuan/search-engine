1. Introduction (å‰è¨€) 
A MEMS vibratory gyroscope normally operates in the resonant mode to boost its sensitivity by 
utilizing the magnified vibration amplitude at resonance. This requires high Q factors and excellent 
frequency matching between the drive and sense oscillators. However, it is difficult to accomplish 
frequency matching between high-Q oscillators, and any design or fabrication deviation can cause a 
significant frequency mismatch. In-operation frequency tuning has been demonstrated to resolve this 
issue [1]. Nevertheless, this increases the operational complexity. Acar et al. also reported on 
employing multiple drive oscillators with different natural frequencies to increase the drive-mode 
bandwidth and, therefore, facilitate the matching [2]. However, the operation frequency has to be 
fixed at the sense-mode resonant frequency, lacking flexibility, and their single-decoupled design 
leads to coupling from the sense to drive units. 
 
2. Design and Fabrication (å…ƒä»¶è¨­è¨ˆèˆ‡è£½ä½œ) 
Our design is drawn in Fig 1. The drive and sense oscillators each are composed of three identical 
movable masses linked together by mechanical coupling beams. This architecture broadens the 
bandwidth of the mechanical frequency response. Similar concepts have been seen in RF MEMS 
filters [3]. The oscillating directions of the drive and sense units are perpendicular to each other. A 2-
DOF U-shape proof mass and two orthogonal sets of decoupling springs, which together provide the 
double-decoupling mechanism, connect the drive and sense oscillators. The drive oscillation (// x 
axis) can be excited by actuating any of the three masses. When the gyroscope experiences a z-axis 
rotation, a Coriolis force (// y axis) is induced and causes the sense unitâ€™s oscillation, whose 
amplitude is proportional to the angular rate of rotation. The drive oscillator is powered by 
electrostatic comb-drive actuators and the same comb design is also used for capacitive sensing at 
the sense unit.

3. EXPERIMENTS 
Fig. 4 is the experimental setup. A lens with a 
4-cm focal length is placed in front of the 632.8-nm 
He-Ne laser. The laser beam is first focused, then 
diverges, and finally hits the sample with an incident 
of 17Â° and a beam diameter of 1.2 cm. The 
retro-reflected light is reflected by the beam splitter, 
and then passes through the imaging lens so that an 
image is formed on the observation plane/screen. 
We first test a catÂ¶s eye array without the smart 
film. The spacing between the upper (micro lens) and 
lower (concave mirror) modules is 0.14 mm. As 
shown in Fig. 5(a), an array of red bright spots, 
resulting from the retro-reflected light from the catÂ¶s 
eye device, can be observed on the observation 
screen. Devices with spacing far off the ideal value or 
some misalignment between the upper and lower 
modules are also tested for comparison. No bright 
spot can be observed as the conditions for 
retro-reflection are not met.

We next experiment on the device with the smart 
film. The smart film is purchased from the Chiefway 
Optronics Co., LTD [4]. The light transmittance is 
>75% under the clear mode (65 V applied voltage) 
and is <6% in the opaque mode (no applied voltage). 
The response time is around 10 msec for switching 
from the clear to opaque mode, and is around 2 msec 
for the reverse switching. The switching of the image 
pattern is demonstrated by Fig. 5(b)-(c). Fig. 5(b) is 
the image when the voltage is applied, and an array 
of red light spots is observed. Once the voltage is 
removed, the smart film becomes opaque and the 
light spots disappear [Fig. 5(c)].

4. CONCLUSIONS 
We have reported a device with an DUUD\RIFDWÂ¶V
eyes with modulability. The switching of the image 
pattern of the retro-reflected light has been 
demonstrated. This device can be used as the optical 
ID tag of an identification system. Future devices will 
incorporate the function of independent control over 
individual catÂ¶s eyes. Thanks to the retro-reflection 
property, such an optical identification system can 
possesses high security and the capability of 
long-distance interrogation/identification.
