This project explores multimedia 
multicasting for All-IP mobile networks. The 
first part of this project proposes an efficient 
multicast mechanism for the PS-domain 
Multimedia Messaging Service (MMS). Our 
multicast approach is implemented based on 
the Cell Broadcast Service architecture. We 
describe the location tracking procedures of the 
multicast members and the multicast message 
delivery procedure. Also, we develop an 
analytic model to investigate the performance 
of our approach. This study indicates that in 
terms of MMS multicast message delivery cost, 
our approach outperforms the previously 
proposed approaches. 
Furthermore, based on the 3GPP multimedia 
broadcast/multicast service architecture, the 
second part of this project proposes an efficient 
QoS-based multicast approach for UMTS. Our 
approach adopts the existing scalable-coding 
technique to provide multiple levels of service 
quality to diverse mobile devices in the UMTS 
system. An analytic model is developed to 
investigate the performance of our approach. 
The numerical results indicated that in terms of 
transmission costs of core/radio networks, our 
approach outperforms the 3GPP 23.246 
Approach. 
The messaging multicast mechanism is a 
ROC patent (Patent No.: 205010), and a US 
patent (Patent No.: 7058413). Also, under the 
NSC support, this work has been published in 
IEEE Transaction on Vehicular Technology, 
53(6):1891-1902, 2004, and presented in IEEE 
Globecom 2005.

In the second part of this project, we propose

UMTS based on MBMS architecture. The

data from a single source entity to multiple

wireless/mobile networks. Furthermore, as

broadcasting and multicasting is considered one

wireless/mobile communication systems.  
3GPP 22.146 [16] has defined MBMS for 
UMTS networks. In 3GPP MBMS, two modes 
of operations are supported. The broadcast 
mode is a unidirectional point-to-multipoint 
transmission of multimedia data from a single 
source entity to all users in a broadcast service 
area. On the other hand, the multicast mode 
only transmits multimedia data to a specific 
group of mobile users (i.e., the multicast 
members). Both the broadcast and multicast 
modes are intended efficiently to use 
radio/network resources, which is achieved by 
the multicast tables of the networks nodes such 
as GGSN, SGSN, and RNC. The multicast 
tables record the distribution of

II.2. Our Proposed Approaches (Approaches IIa 
and IIb) 
The goals of our multimedia multicasting 
approach are (1) to have a single multimedia 
stream source (i.e., no duplication at the 
MBMS source), (2) to transmit multimedia 
streams to all members in the multicast group 
with satisfactory quality, and (3) to utilize 
effectively the resources of core and radio 
networks. To achieve these goals, the existing 
scalable-coding technique is adopted to deliver 
multimedia streams. Figure 14 elaborates the 
basic concept for scalable coding. The scalable 
coding technique utilizes a layered coder to 
produce a cumulative set of layers where 
multimedia streams can be combined across 
layers to produce progressive refinement.  
For example, if only the first layer (or base 
layer) is received, the decoder will produce the 
lowest quality version of the signal. If the 
decoder receives two layers, it will combine the 
second layer (or the enhancement layer) 
information with the first layer to produce 
improved quality. Overall, the quality 
progressively improves with the number of 
layers that are received and decoded. With 
scalable coding, the requirement of 
single-source multimedia streams is fulfilled, 
and all multicast users can decode their 
preferred multimedia packets depending on the 
devices‚Äô capabilities.  
However, effectively utilizing the resources 
of core and radio networks to transmit 
scalable-coded multimedia streams is still a 
challenging issue. We develop two 
trans-mission modes for our scalable-coding 
enabled multimedia multicasting: ‚ÄúPacked‚Äù 
mode Approach IIA and ‚ÄúSeparate‚Äù mode 
Approach IIB. In the packed mode (see Figure 
15), all layered multimedia data for one frame 
are packed into a packet at MBMS source. 
Then these packed packets are sequentially 
delivered in one shared tunnel (between GGSN 
and SGSN, and between SGSN and RNC) and 
then in one shared radio channel to all multicast 
users. Each packed packet consists of one 
frame of 4-layered multimedia data,as shown in 
Figure 15, packets are sent from GGSN to the 
SGSNs (i.e., SGSN1 and SGSN2), the RNCs 
(i.e., RNC1, RNC2, RNC3 and RNC4) and 
then the RAs (i.e., RA1, RA2, RA3, RA5 and 
RA6) where the multicast users reside. Upon 
receipt of 4-layered multimedia data, the 
multicast users select certain layers to decode 
based on their preferences. 
For Approach IIA, our QoS-based multimedia 
multicasting can be easily implemented in 
UMTS networks without any modification of 
the existing GGSN, SGSNs and RNCs. Since 
the GGSN, SGSNs and RNCs are not aware of 
scalable coding and can not differentiate the 
layers of multimedia streams, 4-layered 
multimedia streams must be sent to all 
multicast users regardless of the users‚Äô request. 
This may result in extra resource (i.e., link 
bandwidth and channelization code) usage at 
the core and radio networks. Also, this leads to 
increased power consumption caused by mobile 
devices (e.g., the mobile phone in RA2) 
requesting low-quality multimedia streams.  
Therefore ‚ÄúSeparate‚Äù mode (Approach IIB) is 
developed to improve the transmission 
efficiency of scalable coded multimedia 
streams. Figure 16 shows the scenario of 
‚ÄúSeparate‚Äù mode for scalable-coded 
multimedia multicasting. In Approach IIB,each 
layered multimedia datum is encapsulated in 
one GTP packet, and all GTP packets are 
transmitted through a single tunnel. To deliver 
effectively the scalable coded multimedia 
streams, the GGSN, SGSNs and RNCs must be 
modified to become aware of scalable coding. 
Importantly, these network nodes do not have 
to understand how scalable coding works. They 
only need to differentiate the layers of received 
multimedia streams, which can be 
accomplished through the tag of GTP packet 
headers.

II. QoS-Based Multimedia Multicasting for 
UMTS Networks 
Based on the analytic model in the previous 
section, we use some numerical examples to 
evaluate the performance of the 3GPP 23.246 
Approach I, and QoS-based mul-timedia 
multicasting Approach IIA and Approach IIB. 
Table 4.1 shows the input parameters and their 
values used in our experiments. In our 
experiments, Foreman is used for test sequences, 
and the number of frames (with the size of 
176x144 QCIF) is 400. MPEG-4 FGS and 
MPEG-4 respectively are used for scalable 
coding and non-scalable coding, and Codec 
adopts Microsoft MPEG-4Reference Software 
[22]. Furthermore, uni-truncation (with 
equivalent bit-rate) is used for all enhancement 
layers of I-Frame and P-Frame. Six levels of 
service quality are provided in the experiments. 
For non-scalable coding, the six quality levels are 
defined by 120Kbps, 150Kbps, 180Kbps, 
210Kbps, 240Kbps and 270Kbps bit rates. To 
have similar quality levels (i.e., similar PSNR 
values) for scalable coding, some experiments for 
quality measurement are made. Figure 16 shows 
the experimental results, and the results indicate 
that the bit rate of the based layer (L1) for 
scalable coding is 120Kbps, and the bit rates for 
the accordingly enhancement layers (i.e., L2, L3, 
L4, L5and L6) are 150Kbps, 120Kbps, 105Kbps, 
90Kbps and 75Kbps. 
Based on the bit-rate setting for scalable 
coding derived from Figure 17, Figure 18 
indicates the effect of Œ± (i.e., portion of class 1 
RAs covering diverse mobile devices) on the 
transmission costs CT for Approach I, Approach 
IIA and Approach IIB. In this figure, the CT  value 
for Approach IIA remains the same as Œ± increases 
(i.e., the number of complicated areas increase). 
On the other hand, the increase of Œ± results in the 
increase of CT  for Approach I and Approach IIB. 
Specifically , the increasing rate for Approach I is 
much larger than that for Approach II. 
Furthermore, when Œ±> 46% and Œ±> 32%, 
Approach IIA and Approach IIB have a smaller CT 
than Approach I respectively. From this figure, 
we observe that when all Ras are class 1, 
Approach IIA has the lowest CT . However, when 
Œ± nearly equals 0, the performance of Approach I 
is better than that either Approach IIA or IIB. Also, 
this figure indicates that as t increases from 30ms 
to 90ms, the overhead for Approach IIB decreases 
marginally, and thus CT slightly decreases. 
In order to investigate the effect of the number 
of service quality levels on CT , Figure 19 shows 
CT against Œ± for four service quality levels (i.e., 
L1, L2, L3, L4). Specifically, the bit rates are set 
to 120Kbps, 150Kbps, 180Kbps, 210Kbps for 
non-scalable coding and 120Kbps, 
150Kbps ,120Kbps, 105Kbps for scalable coding. 
In this figure, a similar result with that in Figure
