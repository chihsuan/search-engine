Abstract 
Interior brick-infill partitions are usually considered as non-structural components, and only 
their weight is accounted for in practical structural design. In this study, they are simulated by 
equivalent compression struts for investigating their effect on the progressive collapse potential of an 
earthquake-resistant RC building. Four notional column loss conditions with a total of fourteen 
brick-infill locations are considered. Linear and nonlinear static analyses of the column-removed 
building with and without brick infills are carried out. Analysis results indicate that the flexural 
response under a prescribed loading combination is moderately reduced with the brick infills. More 
apparent reduction may be obtained as the brick infills are placed in the shorter structural bay 
adjacent to the removed column. They may help to reduce the inelastic displacement response 
caused by column loss. However, the collapse resistance of the column-removed building is not 
significantly influenced due to their brittle failure characteristic. For practical engineering, it may be 
reasonably conservative to only consider the weight of brick-infill partitions in the structural analysis. 
Although the brick infills placed in either bay of the beams bridging over the lost column may 
increase the axial tension of the two-span beam, this effect will gradually diminish with the brittle 
failure.

V. DISCUSSION ON THE EFFECT  
From the previous investigation, it appears that the effect of 
the brick infill on reducing the moment demand at the beam end 
of a column-removed building is not as significant as that 
observed from seismic response analyses [10, 16-17]. A major 
reason may be that, under horizontal earthquake excitations, all 
the brick-infill panels with a consistent longitudinal direction 
on a same story may affect the seismic response in the direction. 
However, as the building is subjected to sudden column loss, 
only the brick-infill panels filled in the column-removed bay 
may influence the vertical loading response. Also, a brick-infill 
panel confined by its perimetric beams and columns usually has 
a length-to-height ratio larger than 1. Hence, the shear 
dimension of the brick-infill panel subjected to horizontal 
seismic force is usually larger than that subjected to vertical 
downward loading, as explained in Fig. 6. Therefore, the 
vertical stiffness component of the brick panel is less than its 
horizontal component. Fig. 7 demonstrates the effect of the 
length-to-height ratio on the vertical and horizontal stiffness 
components of a compression strut. It is seen that the vertical 
stiffness contribution is decreasingly small as compared to 
horizontal one as the length-to-height ratio is larger than 1.5.
