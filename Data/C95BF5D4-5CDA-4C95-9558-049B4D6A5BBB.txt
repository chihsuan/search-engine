1 INTRODUCTION
Advances in wireless communications have increased the popularity of mobile devices and
motivated the development of various mobile applications and services, which are having a
profound effect on peopleâ€™s lifestyles. However, reducing the energy consumption of mobile
devices for communications is a major challenge, especially with the rapid growth of data
communications for emerging applications in cloud computing. In recent years, mobile users
have become increasingly addicted to multimedia applications [22], such as video streaming and
IPTV, which are extremely downlink intensive. Such usage behavior leads to significant energy
consumption for data reception [7]. This observation motivates us to study energy-efficient video
multicast in the fourth-generation (4G) wireless systems, such as WiMAX [14] and LTE [1].
The protocols for 4G wireless networks specify the provision of real-time multicast services
to support the delivery of multimedia data to a group of mobile users in an efficient manner. In
a multicast service, multiple users require access to the same video streaming application, and
the base station providing the video content can adaptively change modulation-coding schemes
based on the channel conditions at the user side. This technique, called adaptive modulation and
coding, is employed in most wireless networks. To provide the users with different levels of
video quality, some layer-based video coding techniques, such as scalable video coding (SVC) [11]
and multiple description coding (MDC) [32], which divide a raw video into multiple layers1, have
been developed. However, this system design raises two challenging issues: 1) how to determine
which video layer should be transmitted with which modulation-coding scheme; and 2) how
to allocate appropriate radio resources to transmit the video layers, especially when several
multicast groups are competing for limited resources.
To solve the above issues, a number of studies has been conducted on the resource allocation
problem for video multicast in wireless networks with layer-based coding techniques, e.g.,
[2], [3], [4], [5], [6], [10], [16], [17], [23], [25], [30]. The concept of using layer-based video
coding in wireless multicast services was first raised in [3], but only two modulation-coding
schemes were used for data transmission. In recent years, many researchers have focused on the
problem of video multicast with the consideration of both layer-based video coding and adaptive
modulation and coding. As a result, efficient heuristic algorithms have been developed to allocate
limited radio resources to multiple multicast groups (or video sessions) with the purpose of
optimizing various objective functions as system performance metrics. Popular performance
metrics include the peak signal-to-noise ratio [16], the utility function of the number of video
layers received by a user [30], and the utility function of the received application rates [17],
[23], [25]. Several algorithms that incorporate theoretical analysis have also been proposed.
For example, to maximize the utility function of the application rates received by users, a
polynomial-time algorithm with a constant approximation ratio was proposed in [25], and a
pseudo-polynomial time optimal algorithm was presented in [23]. Furthermore, to maximize
the total utility (i.e., all usersâ€™ satisfaction), a polynomial-time algorithm was developed in [31],
where the difference in the utility derived by the algorithm and the optimal solution was shown
to be tightly bounded. To protect video layers, some algorithms that exploit forward error correction
techniques have been proposed to maximize the peak signal-to-noise ratio [2], to maximize the
total utility representing the subjective video quality gain of user experiences [5], and to minimize
the video distortion [6], [10]. However, the algorithms do not consider energy consumption of
mobile devices.
The resource allocation problem is further complicated by the need for energy efficiency
which is always a concern in mobile devices. Thus, resource allocation schemes designed for
multicast systems should consider reducing the reception time as a primary objective. Recently,
energy-efficient resource allocation in orthogonal frequency division multiplexing (OFDM) systems

4G cellular wireless systems employ orthogonal frequency division multiple access (OFDMA) based
multi-carrier technology. An OFDMA frame consists of symbols in the time domain and sub-
channels2 in the frequency domain, as shown in Figure 1. A symbol and subchannel combination,
referred to as a tile, is the minimum allocable unit that can be modulated and coded individu-
ally3. In a multicast system, the available tiles in a frame are shared by multiple video groups
(and other non-real-time application services [13]), and users requesting the same video simply
join the corresponding group. Consequently, the users in a multicast group may experience
different channel conditions because of the distance to the base station or other factors. In both
WiMAX and LTE, the base station is allowed to adaptively change modulation-coding schemes
(modulation for short) based on the channel conditions at the user side. A user with good
channel conditions (e.g., when close to the base station) can potentially receive the video data at
a higher modulation rate by opting for a higher-rate modulation (such as 64-QAM) or, at least,
a lower-rate modulation (such as QPSK). In contrast, a user with poor channel conditions must
receive the video data at a lower modulation rate in order to tolerate the bit error rate. Because
users report information about their channel conditions, such as signal-to-noise ratios, when
requesting multicast services, the base station is able to determine the appropriate modulation
of the video data provided to each user. In other words, the base station maps, via a certain
modulation, the application rate (kbps) required by users at the application layer to the channel
rate (tiles or specifically tiles per frame) at the physical layer. Note that, given a certain amount
of video data, more tiles are needed to carry it at a lower modulation rate than at a higher rate.
Therefore, the modulation adopted by the base station determines which users can receive a
video and how many tiles are needed to carry it.
If users requesting the same video experience different channel conditions, the following issue
arises. The base station must either adopt a robust modulation to accommodate all the users,
or it must sacrifice the users with poor channel conditions to provide the others with better
video quality. The issue was addressed by introducing layer-based video coding in multicast
services [3], [23], [25]. Layer-based video coding compresses a raw video into several video
layers, and the video quality is progressively refined as the user receives more layers. There are
two popular coding technologies: SVC and MDC. SVC divides a raw video into a base layer and
multiple enhancement layers. The base layer is essential for the reception of video content with
minimal quality, while the enhancement layers progressively improve the video quality. In other
words, an enhancement layer is only effective if the user has received the base layer and all the
other lower enhancement layers. SVC provides a natural means for efficient adaptation of the
video application data rate [26]. However, it is sensitive to the loss of base-layer packets [15] and
relies on some error-resilience mechanisms [27]. In contrast, MDC is capable of error resilience by
design so that layers can be encoded independently (usually with different application rates);
hence decoding one layer does not depend on the other layers. However, redundancies are
inserted intentionally to make the bitstream more resilient to transmission errors [32].
By exploiting layer-based video coding, the base station can apply different modulations to
the layers, so that users receive the video quality appropriate for the channel conditions they
reported initially. Note that an entire layer must use the same modulation and the number of
tiles needed depends on the modulation specified by the base station. Therefore, for an OFDMA
frame, the base station determines which layer of which video should be transmitted with which
modulation. The base station must also determine how to allocate tiles to the layers4. In an
OFDMA-based multicast system, only some symbols in a frame may carry the video layers a user

To complete the proof, we show that two evenly partitioned subsets can be used to derive
a resource allocation whose total energy consumption is no more than n, and vice versa. Since
each subset corresponds to a symbol, and each integer corresponds to the layer for a group,
the assignment of integer ag to a subset implies that the layer for group g occupies ag tiles in
the corresponding symbol. Because every layer is carried in either symbol, every user needs to
receive exactly one symbol. Thus, the total energy consumption is n. On the other hand, if the
total energy consumption is no more than n, then no user can receive two symbols. This implies
that each layer is only carried in one symbol, and the subset can be evenly partitioned by assign-
ing the corresponding integer into the corresponding subset. The existence of a polynomial-time
algorithm for the partition problem implies the same for our problem. We conclude that the
ELVM problem with SVC applied is NP-hard.

6.2 Simulation Results
Figures 5(a) and 5(b) show the impact of the number of multicast groups on the total energy
consumption under SVC and MDC respectively. As expected, in both cases, the total energy
consumption increases as the number of groups increases. This is because more groups result
in more multicast service requests, so the base station has to allocate more tiles, which occupy
more symbols for multicast data transmissions. However, the proposed algorithms are much
more energy-efficient than CONV when the number of multicast groups is large. In other words,
in terms of energy efficiency, Algo 1 and Algo 2 are more effective than CONV as the network
size increases. We have to point out that if there is only one multicast group, the total energy
consumption of Algo 1 and Algo 2 is the same as that of OPT since the proposed algorithms
can be proved to be optimal algorithms for a network system comprised of one single multicast
group. Even for the case where the number of multicast groups is larger than 1, the performance
of the proposed algorithms is close to that of OPT. The results also show that the performance
of Algo 1 and Algo 2 is closer to that of OPT in the case of 6 subchannels than in the case of
15 subchannels. The reason for this phenomenon is that OPT has more selections to allocate the
tiles for each group when there are more subchannels in a symbol.
Figures 6(a) and 6(b) illustrate the impact of the number of available subchannels in a symbol
on the total energy consumption under SVC and MDC respectively. Both figures show that
under Algo 1, Algo 2, and OPT, the total energy consumption decreases as the number of
subchannels increases. This is because more subchannels result in less symbols received by each
user. In contrast, the number of available subchannels does not have a significant impact on the
performance of CONV, because it allocates radio resources uniformly without considering the
energy consumption. Moreover, under Algo 1, Algo 2, and OPT, the decrease in the total energy
consumption is more obvious in the case of 20 groups than in the case of 8 groups. The reason
is that, as the number of subchannels increases, the number of received symbols decreases; and
the more groups in the network, the more obvious reduction in the total number of symbols
received. The figures show that Algo 1 and Algo 2 are more effective than CONV when a symbol
contains more subchannels.
Figures 7(b) and 7(a) show the impact of different user distributions on the total energy
consumption under SVC and MDC respectively. In both cases, the energy consumption under
the near user distribution is less than that under the other user distributions. This result is

that of Algo 2 for MDC. The reason for this phenomenon is that both algorithms derive the
minimum number of tiles required to satisfy the user requirements and then adopt the same

sensitive to packet loss and more retransmissions would be required. However, this does not
imply that MDC is always more energy-efficient than SVC. The redundancy added for error
resilience for MDC would lead to additional energy consumption.
7 CONCLUSION
In this project, we have studied the problem of energy-efficient resource allocation in 4G wireless
networks with the objective of minimizing the total energy consumption of all users when they
receive video multicast data. The most well-known techniques for implementing layer-coded
video are scalable video coding (SVC) and multiple description coding (MDC). We prove that
the problem is NP-hard for both SVC and MDC. First, we propose a 2-approximation algorithm
for the problem with SVC. Then, we show that, unless P = NP , the problem with MDC cannot be
approximated in polynomial time with a ratio better than 2, and propose a pseudo-polynomial
time 2-approximation algorithm to solve the problem. The results of simulation demonstrate
that the proposed algorithms are very effective in reducing energy consumption, compared
to a conventional approach. They can also obtain a feasible solution more efficient than an
optimal algorithm with brute-force search. The efficacy of the proposed algorithms is more
evident in large-scale networks and when the symbols available for multicast services contain
more subchannels.
