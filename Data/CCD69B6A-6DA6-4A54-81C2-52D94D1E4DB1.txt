that specifies the range of the ARMSE lower bound.

Performance Analysis of Energy Detection Based Spectrum Sensing with
Unknown Primary Signal Arrival Time
Jwo-Yuh Wu, Member, IEEE, Chih-Hsiang Wang, and Tsang-Yi Wang, Member, IEEE
Abstractâ€”Spectrum sensing in next-generation wireless cog-
nitive systems, such as overlay femtocell networks, is typically
subject to timing misalignment between the primary transmitter
and the secondary receiver. In this paper, we investigate the
performance of the energy detector (ED) when the arrival time
of the primary signal is modeled as a uniform random variable
over the observation interval. The exact formula for the detection
probability is derived and corroborated via numerical simulation.
To further improve the detection performance, we propose a
robust ED based on the Bayesian principle. Computer simulation
confirms the effectiveness of the Bayesian based solution when
compared with the conventional ED.
Index Termsâ€”Cognitive radio, spectrum sensing, energy de-
tection.

Does More Transmitting Sensors Always Mean
Better Decision Fusion in Censoring Sensor
Networks with An Unknown Size?
Tsang-Yi Wang, Member, IEEE and Jwo-Yuh Wu, Member, IEEE
Abstractâ€”This paper examines the impact of sensor censoring
on the decision fusion performance in networks with an unknown
number of sensors. In performing the decision fusion process, the
fusion center applies the Chair-Varshney test; suitably modified
to take account of the unknown network size. A closed-form
analytical expression is derived for the error probability of the
modified fusion rule. It is shown that reducing the censoring
probability, i.e., allowing a greater number of sensors to transmit
their decisions, does not necessarily improve the decision fusion
performance. Rather, there exists a certain censoring probability
threshold below which increasing the number of transmitting
sensors simply incurs a greater intra-network communication
overhead but without improving the global decision performance.
Our findings establish that the design of energy-efficient local de-
tection rules should commence with the censoring rate threshold.
Hence, it is desirable that the value of this censoring probability
threshold be known in advance. Accordingly, the present study
proposes an efficient method for identifying the censoring prob-
ability threshold value and determining the corresponding local
censoring rule.
Index Termsâ€”Sensor networks, decision fusion, distributed
detection, energy efficiency, censoring sensor.

closed-form analytical expression for the error probability
of the considered fusion rule has been obtained and a low-
complexity method for computing the upper bound has been

decision rule, a censoring probability threshold exists, below

of censoring schemes for WSNs in which the total number
of sensors is unknown should commence from this starting
point since a censoring probability lower than this point
not only yields a poorer performance, but also results in a
greater energy consumption. Accordingly, an efficient method
has been proposed for finding the starting point and the
corresponding local censoring rule. Finally, the simulation
results have shown that the fusion error probability varies as
a convex function of the censoring probability, and thus the
starting point is unique. The proof for this convexity property
will be examined in a future study.

V. CONCLUSIONS 
 
  This paper has presented an original contribution to the study of 
power allocation for robust distributed BLUE in the presence of 
sensing noise variance uncertainty. The proposed design criterion 
in terms of ARMSE has several unique advantages. Firstly, it is 
established that with a small target AMSE the inverse of ARMSE 
is a close approximation to the true AMSE. Given this appealing 
fundamental result, maximization of the ARMSE is thus a justified 
approach for enhancing the estimation quality. Secondly, starting 
from the ARMSE design metric, rigorous analyses are carried out 
to derive an analytically tractable objective function for the design 
of power allocation factors. The obtained solution is in a closed 
form, and is seen to outperform the non-robust design via computer 
simulation.

ABSTRACT
Energy consumption is a vital concern when implementing
distributed decision fusion in most wireless sensor networks.
This paper studies the impact of sensor censoring on the de-
cision fusion performance when the number of sensors is un-
known at the fusion center. The global decision rule adopted
at the fusion center is the Chair-Varshney fusion rule modi-
fied to take account of the unknown network size. It is shown
that under the assumption of equally likely hypotheses, allow-
ing more transmitting sensors does not necessarily yield bet-
ter decision fusion; rather, there exists a censoring probability
threshold below which the increase in the number of active
sensors just incurs more intra-network communication over-
head but will not improve the global decision performance.
Our findings establish that the design of energy-efficient lo-
cal detection rules should commence with the censoring rate
threshold.

1. INTRODUCTION

distributed decision fusion in most wireless sensor networks
(WSNs). It has been shown that significant savings in the
communication overhead can be achieved by utilizing a sen-
sor censoring policy in which the local nodes transmit their
decisions to the fusion center (FC) if they are judged to be
informative, but remain silent if they are not [1â€“5]. In the

â€œsendâ€ region. The present study adopts a similar â€œyes/noâ€

5. CONCLUSIONS
Based on a modified Chair-Varshney fusion rule, this study
has addressed the impact of sensor censoring on the global de-
cision performance when the number of sensors is unknown
to the FC. Under quite mild conditions on local detection per-
formances as well as under the assumption of equally likely
hypotheses, this study has proved the existence of an error
probability threshold, below which more transmitting sensors
are allowed but the proportional increase in the energy ex-
penditure does not improve the global decision performance.
Therefore, the design of energy-efficient censoring decision
fusion should commence from the starting point, since a cen-
soring probability lower than this point not only yields a poorer
fusion result, but also consumes a greater amount of energy.

C. Discussions 
 
1. The transmit power threshold in (3.16) can be adopted 
uniformly across sensors to meet the target performance 
constraint (2.8). The significance of this result lies in that 
the power allocation factor can be analytically computed 
off-line, and can be used once and for all when the 
network is deployed. There is no need to know the 
instantaneous sensing noise variance and the channel state 
information in determining the transmit power. This can 
further reduce the intra-network coordination for power 
scheduling, and thus improves the network-wide energy 
efficiency. 
2. From (3.15), it can be seen that the minimal number of 
sensor nodes oN , as expected, increases with the ARMSE 
threshold D (recall that a large D means better estimation 
accuracy). Also, more sensors are needed if the sensing 
noise variance threshold Î´  gets large. This is intuitively 
reasonable since, with a large Î´ , local sensor 
measurements tend to be severely corrupted by noise, and 
more sensors should be deployed to collect more data for 
smoothening the noise effect. Moreover, from (3.15), oN  
is seen to increase with the heterogeneity factor Î± . An 
interpretation of this result is that, when Î±  is large, the 
variation in the quality of the local sensor data will also 
be large. Hence, more sensors should be used in order to 
collect a sufficient amount of high-quality data for 
realizing the target estimation accuracy. 
3. It can be immediately seen from (3.16) that the power 
threshold 2op , as one may expect, increases with both the 
channel noise variance 2vÏƒ  and the ARMSE threshold D. 
In addition, 2op  decreases with the network size N. 
4. From (3.16), it is easy to see that 2op  increases with both 
Î´  and Î± . To see the rationale behind this phenomenon, 
we first note from (2.1) and (2.5) that the SNR of the local

How Small the Restricted Isometry Constant of the Effective 
Sensing Matrix Can Be?

Abstractâ€”Knowledge of the achievable restricted isometry 
constant (RIC) of the sensing matrix is crucial for assessing the 
signal reconstruction performance of compressive sensing 
systems. In this paper we consider compressive-domain 
interference cancellation via orthogonal projection, and study the 
achievable RIC of the effective sensing matrix, namely, the 
product of the orthogonal projection matrix and the original 
sensing matrix. While existing algebraic based methods resorted 
to the polarization identity to find an upper bound of the 
considered RIC, motivated by geometric interpretations of the 
orthogonal projection and the restricted isometry property we 
derive an improved RIC in a closed form. The proposed solution 
is shown to be tighter than the existing upper bound. Our 
analytical results, and the asserted performance advantages, are 
further evidenced via computer simulations. 
Keywords-Compressive sensing; interference cancellation; 
restricted isometry property (RIP); restricted isometry constant 
(RIC). 
I. INTRODUCTION 
Compressive sensing (CS) is a new technique which 
exploits the sparsity nature inherent in wide classes of real-
world signals so as to facilitate efficient data acquisition, 
storage, and processing [1-3]. Applications of CS have been 
found in various areas, including analog-to-digital converters 
[4], magnetic resonance imaging [2], wireless communications 
[5-7], and sensor networks [8-9], to name just a few. In most 
cases, a CS system can be mathematically described as an 
underdetermined set of linear equations [1-3]. A fundamental 
issue intensively addressed in the CS literature is perfect, or 
stable, signal recovery based on the compressed measurements 
which are typically incomplete or even distorted [1-3]. It is 
well-known that, to guarantee reliable signal reconstruction, 
the sensing matrix must satisfy the so-called restricted 
isometry property (RIP) with a reasonably small restricted 
isometry constant (RIC) [1-3], [10]. Precise knowledge of the 
RIC of the sensing matrix is thus crucial for characterizing the 
achievable signal recovery performance of CS systems. 
Recently, the problem of recovering sparse signal 
contaminated by sparse interference was addressed in [6-7]. 
Specifically, prior to signal recovery from the compressed 
data, the interference in the compressive domain is first 
removed by means of orthogonal projection. Within such a 
â€œcancel-then-recoverâ€ framework, the effective sensing matrix 
is thus the product of the orthogonal projection matrix P and 
the original sensing matrix Î¦  [6]. Hence, the knowledge of 
the RIC of effective sensing matrix Î¦P  is needed to 
characterize the signal recovery performance. A fundamental 
issue that arises in this scenario is thus: Even though the RIC 
of Î¦  is small, the numerical condition of Î¦P  could be 
radically different from that of Î¦ , and the RIC of Î¦P  will be 
larger than that of Î¦ . Actually, to guarantee a small RIC of 
Î¦P , a more restricted bound of the RIC of the original 
sensing matrix Î¦  is required [6]; however, this calls for 
potentially more data measurements [6-7]. Assuming that the 
supports of the signal and interference do not overlap, in [6-7], 
[11] an algebraic based approach based on the RIP in 
conjunction with polarization identity1 was used to derive an 
upper bound of the RIC of Î¦P . However, the upper bound 
obtained via such an approach is known to be the worst-case 
estimate. Investigation of the achievable RIC of Î¦P  in the 
context of orthogonal projection based sparse interference 
cancellation has not been found in the literature yet. 
This paper is aimed at analytically characterizing the RIC of 
the effective sensing matrix Î¦P . With the aid of geometric 
interpretations of orthogonal projection and RIP, a closed-
form formula of the RIC of Î¦P  is derived, and is shown to be 
tighter than the existing algebraic based solution proposed in 
[6-7], [11]. Hence, the established results allow for a more 
accurate evaluation of the signal reconstruction performance 
within the considered â€œcancel-then-recoverâ€ framework. In 
addition, to achieve a target threshold of the RIC of Î¦P , our 
result allows for a less restricted requirement on the RIC of 
original sensing matrix Î¦ : this in turn implies that the 
measurement size can be further reduced. Computer 
simulations are used to evidence the aforementioned 
advantages asserted by the proposed solution.

V. CONCLUSIONS 
 
Interference is typically a limiting factor of the signal 
recovery performance, and interference cancellation has 
become an indispensable mechanism in many real-world 
communication systems. In this paper, we consider the 
orthogonal-projection based interference cancellation in the 
CS framework, and present an original contribution to the 
study of the achievable RIC of the effective sensing matrix. 
By exploiting geometric characteristics of the orthogonal 
projection and the RIP, we derive an analytic RIC which is 
shown to be tighter than an existing estimate obtained using 
the polarization identity. The tighter solution proposed asserts 
that the signal recovery performance can be potentially better 
than that assessed based on the algebraic-based solution. 
Indeed, by computations about a 5 dB improvement in the 2l  
reconstruction error can be achieved when the RIC of Î¦  is 
0.3Î´ â‰ˆ . Moreover, our result supports that the satisfactory 
signal reconstruction quality specified by an RIC threshold of 
Î¦P  can be achieved by a reduced measurement size. 
Particularly, with a Gaussian random sensing matrix, 
computer simulations show that a 21.5%  reduction in the 
amount of measurements merely results in a degradation of the 
recovered signal-to-noise-ratio (SNR) smaller than 0.5 dB.
